# RSW Methodology — Research Notes

*Compiled 2026-04-21. Every factual claim is followed by an inline citation. Direct quotes use double quotes. Where a source does not make a claim, this document says so rather than filling the gap.*

---

## 1. Origin

**Upstream, non-dental source.** The "rocks, pebbles, sand, water / jar" analogy is not native to dentistry. It was popularised by Stephen R. Covey in *First Things First* (1994) and retold as "The Big Rocks of Life." In Covey's telling, "a man stands before a group of high-powered overachievers and pulls out a one-gallon, wide-mouthed mason jar, setting it on a table before placing fist-sized rocks into it… 'If the big rocks don't go in first, they aren't going to fit in later.'" (Source: Appleseeds summary of Covey's "Big Rocks of Life" — https://www.appleseeds.org/Big-Rocks_Covey.htm). Most dental writers who use RSW are re-applying Covey's time-management metaphor to the dental appointment book.

**The dental adaptation is most consistently attributed to Dr. Cathy Jameson (Jameson Management / The Jameson Group).** Jameson is the most-cited originator of the dental version. *Pocket Dentistry*'s chapter on appointment scheduling states: "Dental consultant Cathy Jameson of Jameson and Associates refers to primary, secondary, and tertiary appointments as rocks, pebbles, and sand." (Source: Pocket Dentistry, "12: Appointment Scheduling Strategies" — https://pocketdentistry.com/12-appointment-scheduling-strategies/). Jameson's own writing in *Dentrix Magazine* lays out eight essentials of scheduling, including "schedule a variety of procedures every day: primary, secondary, tertiary, and pre-block for approximately half of the daily goal with primary procedures" (Source: Dentrix Magazine, "Schedule for Productivity, Profitability, and Stress Control" — https://magazine.dentrix.com/schedule-for-productivity-profitability-and-stress-control/). Jameson frames her life's work around this systemisation: "The success of your practice will be in direct proportion to the success of your systems" (Source: Jameson Management — https://jmsn.com/).

**Other consultants who use the terminology.** The three-bucket language (with some variation — "rock/sand/water," "rock/pebble/sand," "primary/secondary/tertiary," "high/medium/low") appears across:

- **John A. Wilde, DDS** in *Dental Economics* explicitly names it "rock, sand, and water. Longer and more productive rocks are appointed first, shorter sand procedures chunked around them, and the day is filled with water such as recalls, denture adjustments, and more." (Source: Dental Economics, "Trouble with your schedule? Set it up for success." — https://www.dentaleconomics.com/practice/systems/article/14210946/trouble-with-your-schedule-set-it-up-for-success).
- **Burkhart Dental Supply** publishes a "Zone Scheduling" guide that maps rock/sand/water to specific CDT codes (Source: Burkhart Dental — https://www.burkhartdental.com/practice-guide/front-office-systems/constructing-zone-scheduling/).
- **ACT Dental (Kirk Behrendt / Heather Crockett)** covers the same concept under "Block Scheduling" in their podcast and hygiene-code content (Source: ACT Dental, Ep. 101 — https://www.actdental.com/101).
- **The Dental A Team (Kiera Dent)** teaches a three-step block-scheduling method that uses the same rocks-first logic, including explicit anti-patterns (Source: The Dental A Team, "#970: 3 Steps To Implement Block Scheduling" — https://www.thedentalateam.com/podcast/970-3-steps-to-implement-block-scheduling/).
- **Productive Dentist Academy (Dr. Bruce B. Baird)** teaches "design every hour of your schedule around production goals — not time," which is the same idea under a different label (Source: PDA Episode 120 — https://productivedentist.com/podcasts/the-productive-dentist-podcast/episode-120-requested-replay-the-secret-to-productive-dental-scheduling/).
- **Bryant Consultants** acknowledges the language is industry-shared: "You may have heard the terms Rocks, sand and water or even High, medium, low as groups for schedule block types" (Source: Bryant Consultants — https://www.bryantconsultants.com/dental-consulting/successfully-implement-block-scheduling-in-your-dental-practice).
- **MGE Management Experts**, **Dentrix Magazine's Work SMART system**, and **RenewDental Consulting** all use the identical three-tier model (Sources: MGE — https://www.mgeonline.com/2023/designing-the-ideal-schedule-for-your-dental-practice-part-1/ ; Dentrix Work SMART — https://magazine.dentrix.com/the-work-smart-scheduling-system/ ; RenewDental — https://www.renewdentalconsulting.com/post/10-proven-strategies-for-mastering-block-scheduling-in-your-dental-practice-a-comprehensive-guide-w).

**Consultants I could not find publishing explicitly on RSW:** Levin Group (Dr. Roger Levin), Wendy Briggs, Linda Miles, and Debra Engelhardt-Nash all teach block or perfect-day scheduling, but none of the sources returned showed them using the rock/sand/water language specifically. This is a negative finding, not proof of absence.

## 2. Mechanics

### Rock

- **What it is:** High-production, long-chair-time, doctor-intensive procedures. Pocket Dentistry cites Jameson: rocks are "procedures that use the cost of a crown or above" (Pocket Dentistry — https://pocketdentistry.com/12-appointment-scheduling-strategies/).
- **Typical procedures:** "Crowns, bridges, implants, and 4-6 composite restorations" (Source: Dental Management Diary — https://dentalmanagementdiary.wordpress.com/tag/rocks/). Burkhart's zone list adds "Multiple Unit Crown & Bridge, Quadrant Dentistry, Cosmetic Procedures (including Orthodontic Starts), Multiple Units of Composite/Resin, Endo" for restorative rocks (Burkhart — https://www.burkhartdental.com/practice-guide/front-office-systems/constructing-zone-scheduling/). Dr. Wilde lists "molar endo, crown and bridge, dentures, veneers, implants, and generally anything that required an hour or more" (Dental Economics — https://www.dentaleconomics.com/practice/systems/article/14210946/trouble-with-your-schedule-set-it-up-for-success).
- **Typical length:** 60 minutes or more. Wilde reserves "three 60-minute blocks — two in the morning and one in the afternoon" for rocks (Dental Economics — same URL above). Teero and The Dental A Team reference 2-hour blocks for crowns (Teero — https://www.teero.com/blog/dental-block-scheduling).
- **Typical dollar value:** Sources avoid fixed ranges because fee schedules vary. The operational anchor is "crown-or-above fee" (Pocket Dentistry — URL above). Practices using a per-hour target commonly model doctor production at **$500+/hour**, e.g., "if a practice assumes the hygienist produces $1,000 per day, the dentist needs to produce $4,000 per day, which divides to $500 per hour for an eight-hour workday" (Source: search digest citing As The Drill Turns / Teero templates — https://asthedrillturns.com/dental-production-schedule-template/ and https://www.teero.com/blog/dental-block-scheduling). At $500/hr, a one-hour rock is ~$500, a two-hour rock ~$1,000, consistent with crown/implant fees.

### Sand

- **What it is:** Medium-production, shorter procedures. Jameson's "pebbles" in Pocket Dentistry: "those that are about half the cost of a crown." (Pocket Dentistry — URL above).
- **Typical procedures:** "Single fillings, extractions, teeth bleaching" (Pocket Dentistry — URL above); "multiple fillings, single crowns, whitening and similar cases" (RenewDental — https://www.renewdentalconsulting.com/post/10-proven-strategies-for-mastering-block-scheduling-in-your-dental-practice-a-comprehensive-guide-w); "fillings and exams" (Answers.com summary — https://www.answers.com/health-conditions/What_is_the_rock_sand_water_scheduling_concept_in_a_dental_office).
- **Typical length:** 20-40 minutes. No source gave a single canonical number; the common pattern is "shorter sand procedures chunked around" rocks (Wilde, Dental Economics — URL above).
- **Typical dollar value:** Roughly half a crown fee per Jameson (Pocket Dentistry — URL above).

### Water

- **What it is:** Non-productive-but-necessary visits — "no out-of-pocket cost to the patient at that appointment" (Pocket Dentistry — URL above).
- **Typical procedures:** "Crown or bridge deliveries, 1-2 composite restorations, post-operative checks, emergencies" (Dental Management Diary — URL above); "post-op checks, crown seats, suture removals" (Answers.com — URL above); "emergency exams, clear aligner or ortho checks, limited exams and denture relines" (RenewDental — URL above).
- **Typical length:** Short — 10-20 minutes, often slotted in 10-minute increments. Dentistry IQ recommends "switching to 10-minute scheduling increments" for granular water placement (Source: Dentistry IQ — https://www.dentistryiq.com/front-office/article/14302659/scheduling-strategies-for-a-more-efficient-practice).
- **Typical dollar value:** $0 or near-$0 to the patient.

### Ratios

No source publishes a single canonical R:S:W ratio. The two rules that appear most often are:

1. **Jameson's "half the daily goal in primary" rule.** "Pre-block your days for approximately half of your production goals" (Source: Dentistry IQ "Universal and Enduring Systems" — https://www.dentistryiq.com/front-office/scheduling/article/16363291/universal-and-enduring-systems-of-a-dental-practice-part-1). Also restated in Jameson's Dentrix Magazine article cited above.
2. **The Work SMART / Dentrix "75% rule."** "Pre-block your schedule to meet at least 75% of your goal with primary care or blocks which are all high production procedures." (Source: Dentrix Magazine "The Work SMART Scheduling System" — https://magazine.dentrix.com/the-work-smart-scheduling-system/). RevenueWell restates this: "75% of your production goal will be protected so that you can do high production crowns, implants, any of your bridge work, even your partials or your dentures" (Source: RevenueWell — https://www.revenuewell.com/article/block-scheduling-productivity).

No source I found prescribes a per-day variation by weekday. Jameson's philosophy is the opposite: "A month that is made up of evenly productive days will be less stressful than a month that is full of high days and low days." (Source: Dentistry IQ, Jameson article — https://www.dentistryiq.com/front-office/scheduling/article/16363540/scheduling-for-productivity-profitability-and-stress-control).

## 3. Placement rules

**Rocks go in the morning.** This is near-universal:

- "High-production visits including crowns, bridges, implants, and complex cases should be scheduled in the morning" (Source: DentalManagers.com — https://www.dentalmanagers.com/blog/mastering-perfect-day-scheduling-strategies/).
- Burkhart's zone schedule assigns rocks to the morning zone and "Fillings, Single-Unit Crowns, Adjustments, Emergencies (Provide Palliative Treatment)" to the afternoon zone (Burkhart — URL above).
- MGE: "scheduling productive procedures in the morning has some clear advantages" and "hitting your production goal by noon" (MGE — URL above).
- The common target: "80% of your restorative production goal is met in the morning, with the remaining 20% in the afternoon" (Burkhart — URL above).

**Why mornings.** Two rationales recur across sources: (1) staff and doctor are sharpest early; (2) if the day falls apart, the goal is already locked in. "If the schedule falls apart during the day, you have still met your goal for the day by prioritizing morning appointments" (DentalManagers.com — URL above).

**Sand goes mid-day / early afternoon.** Wilde's pattern places a third 60-minute rock block in the afternoon, with sand chunked around morning rocks and water filling gaps (Dental Economics — URL above). Bryant suggests "Mid Mornings: Emergency, fillings, delivery appointments; Late Afternoon: Consultations and follow-up" (Bryant — URL above).

**Water goes late afternoon.** "Allocate two to three hours post-lunch for minor and miscellaneous" (search digest citing Curve Dental / RevenueWell — https://www.curvedental.com/dental-blog/dental-block-scheduling-template ; https://www.revenuewell.com/article/block-scheduling-productivity).

**What happens if you front-load the opposite way:** "When pre-blocks for 'rocks' are taken out and filled with 'sand' the daily production that can be achieved is massively reduced." (Dental Management Diary — URL above). Jameson's original warning: "if you start putting things into your schedule without planning ahead and fill it with little pebbles and grains of sand, soon there will be no room for the Big Rocks, and once your schedule is full, you can't get bigger buckets or dump out any of the sand" (Dental Management Diary — URL above, paraphrasing Jameson's application of Covey).

## 4. Production targets

The exact stated rules across the sources:

| Rule | Source | Attributed to |
|------|--------|---------------|
| "Pre-block for approximately half of the daily goal with primary procedures" | Dentistry IQ, Dentrix Magazine | Cathy Jameson |
| "Pre-block your schedule to meet at least 75% of your goal with primary care" | Dentrix Magazine Work SMART | Dentrix/Henry Schein One |
| "75% of your production goal will be protected" | RevenueWell | General consensus |
| "80% of your restorative production goal is met in the morning, with the remaining 20% in the afternoon" | Burkhart Dental | Zone Scheduling guide |
| "Ideally you reach 75% of your daily goal by lunch" | Curve Dental | General consensus |
| "DO NOT give away pre-blocks until 24 hours ahead" | Dentrix Magazine Work SMART | Dentrix |
| "We do not break earlier than 48 hours before they occur" | Dental Economics | John A. Wilde, DDS |

**Doctor day vs hygiene day.** The goal-split convention is: monthly target ÷ working days = daily goal; then subtract hygiene production to get doctor daily goal (Source: MGE — URL above). Dentistry IQ example: "If the monthly goal is $40,000 and you will be available 16 days, that equals $2,500 per day" (Dentistry IQ Jameson — https://www.dentistryiq.com/front-office/scheduling/article/16363540/scheduling-for-productivity-profitability-and-stress-control).

Hygiene-specific goals use different arithmetic — hygienist typically at $1,000/day in the $500/hr dentist model (As The Drill Turns / Teero templates — URLs above). None of the sources explicitly restated the "75% in rocks" rule for hygiene; hygiene's "rocks" are a different category (see Section 5).

## 5. RSW and hygiene

**Yes, the hygiene column is also RSW-scheduled**, using hygiene-specific procedure categories:

**Hygiene "rocks"** per Burkhart: "Implants, Sleep Appliance Treatment, New Patients, Periodontal Therapy (D4341 & D4342), Periodontal Maintenance (D4910)" (Burkhart — URL above). Consensus across sources: new patients (NP), SRP (D4341/D4342), and perio maintenance are the hygiene rocks.

**Hygiene "water"** per Burkhart: "Recare Appointments (D1110), Pedo Prophies, Tissue Checks" — the healthy-adult prophy is explicitly classified as water in the hygiene column (Burkhart — URL above).

**Morning placement:** "Schedule hygiene SRP (periodontal therapy) appointments and periodontal maintenance visits in the mornings when possible" (search digest, multiple sources including Dimensions of Dental Hygiene — https://dimensionsofdentalhygiene.com/questions/standards-for-patient-scheduling/).

**The doctor's hygiene check (the 5-minute exam).** The recommended placement is mid-procedure, not end-of-procedure: "the schedule is most effective when the doctor breaks for hygiene exams at a convenient time rather than waiting until the end of the hygiene procedure, and an exam completed in the middle of the procedure allows more time for the patient to ask questions" (search digest, Dimensions of Dental Hygiene — URL above). This means hygiene checks are scheduled *interruptions* to the doctor's rock-sand-water column, and practices accommodate them by leaving small gaps or by scheduling hygiene checks to land during assistant-only phases of a rock (e.g., while an assistant is seating the patient for the next rock, or during impression set / provisional fabrication).

**Hygiene day volume rule of thumb:** "Try to schedule around two patients needing scaling and root planning in an eight-hour day" (search digest — this balances chair time and hygienist fatigue).

## 6. Multi-column application

**The mix is computed at the doctor level and distributed across columns.** Sources describe 2–3 column setups where the doctor moves between operatories:

- "Two-column scheduling involves using a minimum of two operatories and ideally two dental assistants with staggered appointments in two columns (with a possible third column for overflow)" (Source: D4 Practice Solutions — https://www.d4practicesolutions.com/wp-content/uploads/Harnessing-the-Power-of-the-Dental-Schedule.pdf).
- "In a crown prep example, the dental assistant seats and prepares the patient in the first 30 minutes, the doctor prepares the tooth during the next 60 minutes, and the dental assistant completes the appointment — allowing the doctor to be needed for 60 minutes of a 2-hour crown appointment, which allows staggered scheduling in a second column to increase efficiency" (D4 Practice Solutions — URL above).
- "The doctor's time is typically the tightest constraint in a busy, multi-chair office, with the goal being to plan a schedule allowing the doctor to bounce from room to room, busy but not rushed." (D4 Practice Solutions — URL above).

The Dental A Team describes this as "Sudoku across the board" — the daily R:S:W targets are at the *doctor* level; each column is a sub-sequence that staggers doctor-time rocks against assistant-time setup/cleanup so the doctor's hour is never idle (The Dental A Team, #970 — URL above). Jameson's Dentrix Magazine article makes the same structural point: operatory columns should directly correspond to physical ops, and the scheduler assigns procedures at the doctor level (Work SMART — URL above).

**Practical implication:** a doctor running 3 operatories does NOT try to put three rocks in three columns at 9:00 a.m. — the rocks are staggered so that doctor-time for column A (crown prep) lands while column B is in assistant-setup and column C is in assistant-cleanup or hygiene check.

## 7. Anti-patterns

Consolidated across sources — things the literature explicitly warns against:

1. **Never schedule two rocks back-to-back in the same column where the doctor can't see them.** Kiera Dent (The Dental A Team): "I'm not doing two crowns back to back where I can't see it" (Source: The Dental A Team, #970 — URL above).
2. **Never place an implant and a filling in adjacent columns.** "I'm not putting implants and fillings next door to each other" — the risk is that the small procedure's timing collapses into the large one (The Dental A Team — URL above).
3. **Never give away a pre-block early.** "DO NOT give away pre-blocks until 24 hours ahead" (Dentrix Work SMART — URL above); Wilde's threshold is 48 hours (Dental Economics — URL above).
4. **Never fill the jar with sand first.** Jameson's core warning: once the small stuff is in, the rocks no longer fit (Dental Management Diary — URL above).
5. **Never put a rock right after lunch without buffer.** Implied across "80% of production in the morning" guidance; nothing productive should be left to salvage the afternoon (Burkhart; MGE — URLs above).
6. **Never leave block enforcement un-owned.** Dentrix Work SMART: "Assign a 'scheduling hawk' to review schedules daily (a.m. and p.m.)" (Dentrix — URL above). MGE: "one person needs to ultimately be in charge of it" (MGE — URL above).
7. **Never treat block scheduling as set-it-and-forget-it.** Multiple sources (Yapi, RenewDental — URLs above) flag this: the schedule needs continuous tuning as fee schedules, staffing, and procedure mix change.

A claim I sometimes see but **could not source** with a direct quote: "never let an assistant run a rock's setup alone." This may be practice lore rather than published guidance.

## 8. Criticisms and limitations

Published pushback on RSW / block scheduling is mild — no consultant in the sources I found rejects the method outright. The limitations most commonly surfaced are:

- **Rigidity hurts when emergencies hit.** "A rigid schedule could cause delays or force you to rush other appointments when emergency cases walk in without flexibility. It's crucial to have buffer time to handle unexpected situations." (Source: Yapi — https://yapiapp.com/blog/dental-appointment-scheduling/dental-block-scheduling-template/).
- **High implementation cost.** "Substantial planning and team training are required, and if done poorly, you might face delays or miscommunications." (Yapi — URL above). The Dental A Team gives a quantified implementation timeline: "Allow 6-8 weeks for stabilization before adjustments… Hygiene blocks require 6-month implementation timeline" (#970 — URL above).
- **Ongoing monitoring is non-trivial.** "Block scheduling is not a set-it-and-forget-it process and requires constant evaluation and adjustment." (Yapi — URL above).
- **Team resistance.** "Ignoring valuable input from your team can be a major roadblock… failing to gather feedback and address concerns can lead to resistance and resentment towards the new system, manifesting in decreased productivity and poor communication." (Yapi — URL above).
- **Upstream criticism of the big-rocks philosophy.** Outside dentistry, Renee Fishman argues "Steven Covey's Big Rocks First Strategy is Wrong" because it still treats time as a container to fill rather than attention to direct (Source: My Meadow Report — https://mymeadowreport.com/reneefishman/2017/big-rocks-first/). This is philosophical rather than operational, and no dental source has carried the argument into the clinical scheduling context that I could find.

**What sources agree RSW does *not* solve:** no-show economics, same-day cancellations, PPO fee-schedule compression, and clinical capacity ceilings. RSW optimises the *shape* of the day; it does not add chair time or raise fees.
