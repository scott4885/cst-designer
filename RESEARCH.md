## The Rock-Sand-Water Framework

This is THE foundational concept in dental scheduling. Think of it like filling a jar:

### ROCKS = High-Production Procedures (schedule FIRST, in the MORNING)
- **Doctor:** Crown preps, multi-unit bridge work, implant placement, quadrant dentistry, cosmetic procedures (veneers), orthodontic starts, multi-unit composites, endodontics
- **Hygiene:** SRP (Scaling & Root Planing / D4341 & D4342), Periodontal Maintenance (D4910), New Patient comprehensive exams, implant maintenance, sleep appliance treatment
- **Why morning:** Doctor and patient energy highest, complex procedures need focus, if treatment runs long there's afternoon buffer
- **Production value:** These are $800-$5,000+ procedures

### SAND = Medium-Production Procedures (schedule in GAPS and EARLY AFTERNOON)  
- **Doctor:** Single fillings, single-unit crowns, limited treatments
- **Hygiene:** Adult prophy/recare (D1110), pedo prophies
- **Production value:** $150-$800 per procedure

### WATER = Low-Production / Non-Productive (schedule LAST, LATE AFTERNOON)
- **Doctor:** Crown/veneer seats (cementation), adjustments, denture deliveries, consultations, post-op checks, emergency palliative treatment, oral surgery extractions
- **Hygiene:** Tissue checks, sealants, fluoride treatments, Botox
- **Production value:** $0-$300 per procedure

### Key Insight: 80/20 Morning/Afternoon Split
**80% of restorative production goal should be met by lunch.** The afternoon is for completing work, handling emergencies, and lower-production procedures. This protects against cancellations and no-shows (which are more common in the afternoon).

## Zone Scheduling (Two-Column Doctor Scheduling)

### The Concept
The doctor works between 2-3 operatories with staggered scheduling. The dental assistant handles setup, anesthesia wait time, temporaries, and cleanup — the doctor only needs to be in the chair for the actual clinical portion.

### Staggered Timing Breakdown (per Burkhart)
For a crown prep (120 min total appointment):
```
Assistant Time (first 30 min): Seat patient, take vitals, review treatment, administer anesthesia, wait for numbness
Doctor Time (next 60 min): Prepare tooth, take impression, select shade
Assistant Time (last 30 min): Fabricate/place temporary, home care instructions, dismiss patient, chart notes, operatory turnover
```

This means the doctor is only needed for 60 of 120 minutes. During the other 60 minutes, the doctor can be in the OTHER operatory doing clinical work.

### Two-Column Schedule Example
```
Column 1 (OP1)           Column 2 (OP2)
8:00 [A] Crown Prep     8:00 [empty - no patient yet]
8:30 [D] Crown Prep     8:30 [A] Filling setup
9:00 [D] Crown Prep     9:00 [D] Filling  
9:30 [A] Crown Temp     9:30 [D] Filling
10:00 [A] Crown Dismiss  10:00 [A] Filling dismiss
```

### Matrixing Codes (D/A/H)
- **D** = Doctor actively in this operatory
- **A** = Assistant working (doctor NOT present, doing setup/cleanup/temps)
- **H** = Hygienist working independently
- These codes tell the front desk WHO is in the room and help with scheduling conflicts

## Doctor-Hygiene Integration (Matrixing)

### The Flow
The doctor "floats" to hygiene operatories for exams during natural breaks in restorative work:

1. **Hygienist starts recare appointment** (cleaning, x-rays, perio charting) — takes ~40 min
2. **Doctor breaks from restorative** during assistant-only time
3. **Doctor does hygiene exam** — takes 10-20 min (exam, review findings, discuss treatment)
4. **Doctor returns to restorative** operatory
5. **Hygienist finishes** (fluoride, home care, dismiss) — takes ~10 min

### Best Practice: Mid-Procedure Exams
The doctor should examine hygiene patients **mid-appointment** (not at the end). This:
- Gives patient time to ask questions
- Lets hygienist reinforce doctor's recommendations
- Prevents end-of-appointment bottleneck where doctor is finishing restorative AND all hygiene patients need exams simultaneously
- Increases case acceptance

### Timing Hygiene Exams
- Schedule hygiene SRP and perio maintenance in the **morning** when possible — these patients often DON'T need doctor exams, freeing the doctor to focus on restorative
- Schedule recare (prophy) patients when the doctor has natural **assistant-only gaps** in their restorative schedule

## Production Goal Calculations

### The 75% Rule
Schedule block minimums that total **75% of the provider's daily production goal.** This is NOT 75% capacity — it's 75% of the dollar target.

### Calculation Example
```
Doctor Daily Goal: $5,000
75% Target: $3,750

Block Distribution:
- 2x HP blocks (Crown prep, $1,200 each) = $2,400
- 1x HP block (Multi-unit composite, $900) = $900
- 1x NP consult ($300) = $300
- 1x Emergency access ($150) = $150
TOTAL SCHEDULED: $3,750 ✅

Remaining 25% ($1,250) comes from:
- Hygiene exam fees collected during matrixing
- Additional production from NP same-day treatment
- Fill-in production from quick-call list
- Walk-in emergencies
```

### Hourly Production Rate
```
Doctor working 7:00-6:00 (11 hours - 1 hour lunch = 10 hours)
Goal: $5,000 / 10 hours = $500/hour

This means every 10-min slot represents ~$83 of expected production
```

### Template B: 2 Doctors + 3 Hygienists (Large Practice)
Same principles but:
- Each doctor has their own 2-column OP rotation
- Hygienists stagger SRP/PM in morning across different time slots
- Doctor hygiene exams are split: Dr. 1 covers HYG 1 & 2, Dr. 2 covers HYG 3
- More NP slots (2-3 per day vs 1-2)

### Template C: Solo Doctor, 1 Hygienist (Small Practice)
- Doctor works single-column (no staggering)
- Blocks are back-to-back
- Hygienist works independently, doctor does exams between patients
- Simpler scheduling but same Rock-Sand-Water principle

## Emergency/Access Block Strategy

### Placement Rules
- Reserve 1-2 ER/Access blocks per day
- Best times: **10:00-10:30 AM** and **2:30-3:00 PM** (mid-session)
- If no emergency presents by the morning, front desk fills with production from quick-call list
- If no emergency by afternoon slot, fill with same-day treatment or quick appointments

### Hold Time
- Hold emergency blocks for 2-3 days before releasing them
- As the day approaches, if no emergency is scheduled, release the block for other production

## Scheduling Rules Summary for the Algorithm

### Priority Order for Block Placement
1. **LUNCH** — Block first (immovable)
2. **HP/ROCK blocks** — Place in morning, largest production first
3. **NP blocks** — Place in first or second morning slot
4. **SRP blocks** — Place in hygiene morning slots
5. **ER/Access blocks** — Reserve mid-morning and mid-afternoon
6. **MP/SAND blocks** — Fill mid-morning gaps and early afternoon
7. **NON-PROD/WATER blocks** — Fill late afternoon
8. **Recare** — Fill remaining hygiene slots throughout day

### Constraint Rules
1. No procedure should cross the lunch boundary
2. HP blocks should not start after 3:00 PM (won't finish in time)
3. NP consults should be in first 2 hours of the day
4. SRP should be morning when possible (no exam needed = doctor can focus)
5. Crown seats/deliveries always afternoon (lab work arrives midday)
6. Last block of day should end 30-60 min before close (cleanup/admin)
7. Doctor matrixing: plan hygiene exams during assistant-only time windows
8. Production minimums: total block values ≥ 75% of daily goal
9. Each hygienist should have at least 1 SRP or PM per day (morning)
10. Double-booked doctors need assistant-only time staggered between operatories

### Production Distribution (Target Percentages)
- HP blocks: 55-70% of 75% target
- NP blocks: 5-10% of 75% target
- MP blocks: 15-25% of 75% target
- ER/Access: 5-10% of 75% target
- NON-PROD: 0-5% (often $0 production)

## What Makes a "Perfect Day" Schedule

1. **Morning loaded with production** — 80% of goal met by lunch
2. **No dead time** — Every slot either has a patient or is strategically held
3. **Variety** — Mix of procedure types keeps the team engaged
4. **Buffer time** — Small gaps between major procedures for catch-up
5. **Emergency access** — Reserved slots that can be filled if unused
6. **Doctor flow** — Smooth transitions between operatories and hygiene exams
7. **Patient experience** — Minimal wait times, appointment starts on time
8. **End-of-day grace** — Last 30 min for admin, not starting new procedures
